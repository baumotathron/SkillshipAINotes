MODULE 3: DEFINING PROBLEMS & MARKET RESEARCH

Master the frameworks that separate great PMs from average ones

Week 3 Problem Prioritization Market Research

Introduction

Imagine you're a PM at Swiggy, and users are complaining about delivery delays. Your CEO wants this "fixed immediately." But here's the thing ‚Äì should you solve delivery delays for the 5% of orders during peak rain, or the payment failures affecting 15% of users daily? Without a systematic approach, you'll end up playing whack-a-mole with problems, building features nobody really needs, and burning through your team's time and energy.

This is why problem definition and market research form the foundation of product management. They're not just academic exercises ‚Äì they're the difference between building the next Zomato and the next TinyOwl (remember them?).

Why This Module Matters for PMs

As a product manager, you're essentially a professional problem-solver. But here's what separates great PMs from average ones:

  • Great PMs solve the RIGHT problems ‚Äì the ones that matter most to users and business
  • Average PMs solve the LOUDEST problems ‚Äì whatever the CEO or vocal customers are shouting about

Consider Paytm's journey. In 2015, they could have focused on 50 different problems: Better UX, more payment options, faster transactions, loyalty programs, or merchant onboarding. But they prioritized demonetization readiness ‚Äì a problem most competitors ignored. When demonetization hit in November 2016, Paytm was ready while others scrambled. That singular focus on the RIGHT problem made them the dominant player.

What Happens When You Skip This Step

Case Study: Google+ (The $585 Million Mistake)

What they built: A Facebook competitor with circles, hangouts, and social features

What they assumed: "People want better privacy controls in social media"

What they didn't research: Whether users would switch from Facebook for privacy alone

The reality: Users wanted convenience and network effects, not just privacy

Result: Shut down in 2019 after burning $585 million

Indian Example: TinyOwl Food Delivery

What they built: Food delivery with focus on SMS-based ordering

What they assumed: "Users want to order food via SMS instead of apps"

What they didn't research: User preference for visual menus and app-based ordering

The reality: Users wanted to SEE food photos, not just read SMS menus

Result: Acquired by Roadrunnr (later shut down). Swiggy won with visual, app-first approach

Both companies had talented teams, good funding, and strong execution. But they built solutions for problems that either didn't exist or weren't important enough for users to change their behavior.

Connection to Product vs Service Module

Remember from Module 2 that products scale by standardization while services win through personalization? The same logic applies to problems:

  • Product problems affect many users in similar ways (Netflix buffering, Zerodha app crashes)
  • Service problems are often individual and contextual (Urban Company's beautician is late for one customer)

Your problem prioritization framework needs to account for this. A problem affecting 1000 product users might be more important than a service issue affecting 10 clients, even if those 10 clients are very vocal.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you'll be able to:

  1. Problem Matrix: Score and prioritize problems using Spread, Severity, and Frequency dimensions
  2. Market Research: Conduct both opportunity research (is there a market?) and validation research (will they use my solution?)
  3. TAM-SAM-SOM: Calculate realistic market size and set achievable user acquisition targets
  4. Integration: Use all three frameworks together to make confident go/no-go decisions on product ideas

Think of these as your PM toolkit for the most critical question in product management: "What should we build next, and why?"

Section 3.1: The Problem Matrix

üéØ Tutor Instructions

Opening Question: "Imagine you're the PM at Zomato, and you get these complaints in one day: app crashes on older phones, vegetarian filter doesn't work, delivery tracking is inaccurate, restaurant photos are outdated, and payment takes too long. You can only fix one this month. Which one and why?"

Analogy: "This is like a hospital emergency room. Not all patients can be treated first-come, first-served. You need triage based on urgency, severity, and resources required."

3.1.1 Why We Need a Problem Matrix

Let's start with a real scenario that every PM faces. You're the product manager at Swiggy, and your support team forwards you this week's top user complaints:

  • "App crashes when I try to reorder from history" (47 complaints)
  • "Can't find restaurants that deliver to my office" (23 complaints)
  • "Delivery partner called, but I couldn't understand their language" (156 complaints)
  • "Food arrived cold because tracking was wrong" (89 complaints)
  • "Can't split payment between card and wallet" (12 complaints)

Your engineering team can fix exactly ONE of these problems in this sprint. Which one do you choose?

Your gut might say "the one with most complaints" (language barrier with delivery partners). But what if those 156 complaints came from just 20 users who are very vocal on social media? What if the reorder crash affects 40% of your power users who order daily, but they're not complaining ‚Äì they're just quietly switching to Zomato?

This is why we need objective prioritization. The Problem Matrix framework helps you move beyond gut feelings and vocal minorities to make data-driven decisions about which problems truly deserve your team's attention.

The Cost of Solving Wrong Problems First

üìä Real Example: Paytm's 2017 Priority Mistake

After demonetization, Paytm had two major problems to solve:

  • Problem A: App crashes during peak payment hours (affected 30% of users, happened daily during 7-9 PM)
  • Problem B: Difficulty adding money to wallet via net banking (affected 5% of users, but they were high-value customers)

What they prioritized: Problem B (because high-value customers were calling and complaining)

What happened: Lost millions of regular users to PhonePe and Google Pay during peak hours when the app crashed

Learning: Vocal, high-value customers aren't always your biggest problem

When you solve the wrong problem first, you face a triple penalty:

  • Opportunity Cost: You didn't solve the right problem, so users are still frustrated
  • Resource Waste: Your engineering team spent weeks on something with limited impact
  • Time Delay: The real problem gets worse while you're distracted

Objective vs Subjective Prioritization

Most PMs prioritize problems subjectively:

  • "The CEO mentioned this in the meeting" (HiPPO syndrome)
  • "This user tweeted about it" (squeaky wheel syndrome)
  • "I personally face this problem" (personal bias)
  • "This seems technically interesting" (engineering bias)

The Problem Matrix forces you to be objective by scoring every problem on the same three dimensions: Spread (who's affected), Severity (how much it hurts), and Frequency (how often it happens).

3.1.2 The Three Dimensions Explained

SPREAD (Who is affected?)

Definition: How many users or customers face this problem as a percentage of your total user base.

Spread is about scope, not absolute numbers. A problem affecting 10,000 users sounds big, but if you have 10 million users, that's only 0.1% spread ‚Äì relatively small.

Spread Scoring Guide (1-5 scale):

  • 1 = Very few users (< 1% of user base)
  • 2 = Small segment (1-5% of users)
  • 3 = Moderate segment (5-20% of users)
  • 4 = Large segment (20-50% of users)
  • 5 = Majority/nearly all users (>50%)
üì± Real Examples with Spread Scoring:

Swiggy: "Can't find vegetarian filter"

  • Analysis: 40% of Indian population is vegetarian, and most Swiggy users are urban Indians
  • Data source: User surveys + usage analytics
  • Spread Score: 4 (affects large segment - 40% of users)

Zerodha: "App crashes on iOS 14"

  • Analysis: Only 3% of Zerodha users use iOS 14 (most use Android or older iOS)
  • Data source: Device analytics from app crashes
  • Spread Score: 2 (affects small segment - 3% of users)

Urban Company: "No AC repair option"

  • Analysis: 15% of users search for AC services, mostly seasonal (summer months)
  • Data source: Search queries and seasonal patterns
  • Spread Score: 3 (affects moderate segment - 15% of users)
How to Measure Spread

You need data, not guesses. Here's how to get it:

  • User Analytics: How many users hit the problematic feature/flow?
  • Support Tickets: What percentage of users report this issue?
  • Surveys: Ask a representative sample if they face this problem
  • User Interviews: Deep-dive with 15-20 users across different segments
  • A/B Tests: Test with a small group to measure impact
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake: Confusing Vocal Minority with Actual Spread

Scenario: 100 users tweet angrily about a missing feature

Wrong conclusion: "This affects lots of users" (because they're loud)

Right approach: Check if those 100 represent 100 out of 1,000 users (10% spread) or 100 out of 100,000 users (0.1% spread)

Key learning: Volume of complaints ‚â† Spread. Always measure against your total user base.

SEVERITY (How much does it hurt?)

Definition: The impact on a user when they encounter this problem. Think emotional pain + functional disruption.

Severity isn't just about whether the app works or not. It's about how much the problem disrupts the user's intended goal and their emotional state.

Severity Scoring Guide (1-5 scale):

  • 1 = Minor annoyance (user barely notices, continues using)
  • 2 = Slight inconvenience (user finds workaround, mild frustration)
  • 3 = Moderate pain (user frustrated but continues using)
  • 4 = Major pain (user considers leaving, significant frustration)
  • 5 = Critical (user cannot complete task/loses money/data)
üíî Real Examples with Severity Scoring:

Swiggy: "App crashes during payment"

  • Functional impact: User cannot complete their order
  • Emotional impact: Frustration, anxiety (especially if hungry), loss of trust
  • Business impact: Lost order, user might switch to Zomato
  • Severity Score: 5 (critical - task cannot be completed)

Paytm: "Wrong font color in settings menu"

  • Functional impact: None - all features work normally
  • Emotional impact: Barely noticeable, maybe looks slightly unprofessional
  • Business impact: Zero
  • Severity Score: 1 (minor annoyance)

Zerodha: "Stock prices delayed by 2 seconds"

  • Functional impact: Traders make decisions on old data
  • Emotional impact: High stress, feeling of losing money
  • Business impact: Users lose money, might switch to other trading apps
  • Severity Score: 4 (major pain - financial loss possible)
Emotional vs Functional Severity

Don't underestimate emotional severity. Sometimes a "small" functional problem creates massive emotional pain.

Case Study: Instagram Story Upload Failure
  • Functional impact: Medium - users can still post to feed, just not Stories
  • Emotional impact: HIGH - users feel excluded from real-time social sharing
  • Why emotional severity matters: Stories are about "in-the-moment" sharing. When it fails, users feel they've lost that moment forever. This emotional pain drives them to Snapchat or other platforms immediately.

Learning: Consider the CONTEXT of the user's goal, not just the technical impact

B2B vs B2C Severity Differences

The same problem can have different severity in different contexts:

Problem B2C Context B2B Context Why Different?

(User tries again later)		 Severity: 4-5

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake: PMs Underestimate Emotional Severity

Example: Dating app photo upload fails occasionally

PM thinking: "It's just a photo, they can try again" (Severity = 2)

User reality: "I saw someone I wanted to match with, uploaded my best photo to impress them, and it failed. Now they're gone from my feed forever" (Severity = 4)

Key learning: Always consider the user's emotional state and context when scoring severity

FREQUENCY (How often does it happen?)

Definition: How often users encounter this problem. This includes both individual frequency (how often each user hits it) and aggregate frequency (how many users hit it daily across your entire base).

Frequency Scoring Guide (1-5 scale):

  • 1 = Rare (once a year or less per user)
  • 2 = Occasional (few times a year per user)
  • 3 = Regular (monthly per user)
  • 4 = Frequent (weekly per user)
  • 5 = Very frequent (daily or every use)
‚è∞ Real Examples with Frequency Scoring:

Swiggy: "Can't reorder from history"

  • Individual frequency: Power users want to reorder 2-3 times per week
  • Usage context: When users find a restaurant they like, they reorder frequently
  • Frequency Score: 4 (frequent - weekly need for affected users)

Ola: "Cab cancellation after booking"

  • Individual frequency: Happens to each user once every month on average
  • Usage context: Peak hours, bad weather, remote locations
  • Frequency Score: 3 (regular - monthly occurrence)

Amazon: "Cannot edit review after posting"

  • Individual frequency: Most users never need to edit reviews; those who do need it rarely
  • Usage context: Only when user makes mistake or product changes after review
  • Frequency Score: 1 (rare - once a year or less)
Usage Patterns Matter

The same problem can have different frequency for different user segments:

Case Study: Zerodha "Portfolio sync lag"
  • For casual investors: Check portfolio once a week ‚Üí Frequency = 3
  • For active traders: Check portfolio 50 times a day ‚Üí Frequency = 5

PM Decision: Even if casual investors are 80% of users, if active traders generate 60% of revenue, you weight the frequency toward the active trader experience.

Key learning: Consider both user volume AND business value when scoring frequency

Segment-Specific Frequency

Always ask: "Frequency for which users?" Different segments use your product differently:

User Segment Swiggy Usage "Delivery Tracking Inaccurate" Frequency
Daily orderers Order 7x/week Score: 5 (hit it daily)
Weekend orderers Order 2x/week Score: 4 (hit it weekly)
Occasional orderers Order 2x/month Score: 3 (hit it monthly)
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake: Global Average Hides Power User Pain

Example: "App logout" problem

  • Average frequency: 0.1 times per month per user (looks low)
  • Hidden reality: 5% of users (power users) get logged out 5 times per day
  • Why this matters: Those 5% might be your most valuable users who generate 40% of revenue

Right approach: Segment frequency analysis, not just global averages

3.1.3 Calculating the Total Priority Score

Once you've scored each problem on all three dimensions, calculating priority is simple:

Priority Score = Spread + Severity + Frequency

  • Maximum possible score: 15 (5+5+5)
  • Minimum possible score: 3 (1+1+1)

Priority Interpretation Guide

Score Range Priority Level Action Timeline
13-15 Critical Priority Drop everything, fix immediately This sprint
10-12 High Priority Plan for next sprint Within month
7-9 Medium Priority Add to next quarter roadmap Within 3 months
4-6 Low Priority Add to backlog When convenient
3 Ignore Don't solve (or delete from backlog) Never

Why This Scoring System Works

The beauty of adding (not multiplying) the three dimensions is that it balances different types of problems:

  • High spread, low severity: Many users affected slightly (like cosmetic bugs)
  • Low spread, high severity: Few users affected badly (like edge case crashes)
  • High frequency compensates for lower spread/severity: Small problems that happen daily add up

3.1.6 Common Mistakes PMs Make

Mistake 1: HiPPO Syndrome (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)

Scenario: CEO walks into your sprint planning meeting

CEO: "That dark mode color in our app looks terrible. Fix it this sprint."

What PM typically does: Inflates the score to justify CEO's request

  • Claims spread is higher than it is
  • Argues it affects "brand perception" (inflated severity)
  • Pushes it to critical priority

What PM should do:

  • Score it objectively (likely 1-1-4 = 6, low priority)
  • Show CEO the Problem Matrix with all scored problems
  • Say: "Happy to fix this, but here's what we'd delay. Are you okay with that trade-off?"
  • Educate CEO on the framework over time

How to avoid: Make the scoring process transparent and collaborative. Show your work.

Mistake 2: Squeaky Wheel Gets the Grease

Scenario: 500 users flood Twitter with complaints about a feature

What it looks like: Massive user outcry, trending hashtags, PR team panicking

What PM typically assumes: High spread because so many people are complaining

The reality check: 500 vocal users out of 10 million total users = 0.005% spread

Why this happens:

  • Vocal users are often power users (they care more)
  • Social media amplifies complaints
  • Silence doesn't mean satisfaction

How to avoid: Always measure actual spread with data, not complaint volume

Mistake 3: Ignoring Compound Frequency

Scenario: Individual users face a problem only once per month (frequency = 3)

PM thinking: "Low frequency, not urgent"

Hidden calculation: 1 million users √ó once per month = 33,333 problem occurrences daily

Aggregate impact: Massive - one of your users faces this problem every 2.6 seconds

Why PMs miss this: They think about individual user experience, not aggregate experience

How to avoid: Calculate total occurrences = Number of affected users √ó Individual frequency

Mistake 4: Personal Bias

Common biases affecting PM scoring:

  • Platform bias: PM uses iOS, so iOS bugs seem more severe
  • Feature bias: PM built a feature, so problems with that feature seem less severe
  • Technical bias: PM finds technical problems more interesting than UX problems
  • Personal use bias: PM doesn't use certain features, so problems there seem less important

Example: PM who doesn't use dark mode consistently underscores dark mode problems

How to avoid:

  • Always score from user perspective, not your own
  • Use data to validate your scores
  • Get second opinions from other team members
  • Do regular user interviews to stay connected to different user segments

Section 3.2: Market Research

3.2.1 What is Market Research and Why Does It Matter?

üèÜ Success Story: How Zepto Cracked Quick Commerce

The Observation (2021): Founders noticed quick commerce (10-minute delivery) working in China and Korea

The Question: "Will Indians pay a premium for 10-minute grocery delivery, or do they prefer cheap delivery even if it takes 2 days?"

Research Phase 1 - Secondary Research:
  • Read reports on Indian e-commerce behavior
  • Analyzed BigBasket and Grofers delivery patterns
  • Found gap: No one doing sub-30 minute delivery
Research Phase 2 - Primary Research:
  • Surveyed 1000 urban customers in Mumbai
  • Key finding: 65% said "Yes" to premium pricing for essentials (milk, medicines, baby food)
  • Willingness to pay: ‚Çπ30-50 delivery fee for 10-minute speed
  • Trigger moments: "ran out of milk for morning tea," "baby needs diapers at night"
Research Phase 3 - MVP Testing:
  • Started operations in just 2 neighborhoods in Mumbai
  • Measured: Order frequency, repeat rate, complaints
  • Result: 40% repeat rate in Week 1, 60% by Week 4

Decision Point: Only AFTER validating demand did they raise ‚Çπ60 crore and scale to 10 cities

Outcome: ‚Çπ2000+ crore GMV in Year 2, successful validation of quick commerce in India

What Happens When You Skip Market Research?

For every Zepto success story, there are dozens of failures that could have been avoided with proper research:

  • Build the wrong thing: 6 months of engineering wasted on features nobody wants
  • Target the wrong users: Marketing budget spent reaching people who'll never convert
  • Price it wrong: Either leave money on the table or price yourself out of market
  • Run out of runway: Burn cash while figuring out what users actually want
  • Miss the timing: By the time you pivot, competitors have captured the market

3.2.2 Two Types of Market Research

Not all market research serves the same purpose. You need different approaches for different questions:

TYPE 1: OPPORTUNITY RESEARCH

"Is there a market for my idea?"

Use this when:
  • You have a product idea but don't know if there's demand
  • Entering a new market or geography
  • Exploring a new user segment
  • Deciding between multiple product ideas
What you're researching:
  • Market size: TAM, SAM, SOM (covered in Section 3.3)
  • User needs: What problems do they have? How painful are they?
  • Existing solutions: What do people use today? Where are the gaps?
  • Competition landscape: Who else is solving this? How well?
  • Market trends: Is this market growing/shrinking? What's driving change?
üì± Example: Cred's Opportunity Research (2018)

Market Opportunity Research:

  • Market size: 50M credit card holders in India (TAM)
  • Target refinement: Only 20M pay on time consistently (SAM - their target audience)
  • Market growth: Credit cards growing 25% annually

User Need Research: Interviewed 200 credit card users across 6 cities

  • 75% forget payment dates (despite smartphone reminders)
  • 60% don't track or redeem reward points
  • 80% find bank apps confusing for credit card management
  • 45% have missed payments despite having money

Competition Analysis:

  • Bank apps: Clunky, not focused on credit cards, poor UX
  • Paytm: Has credit card bill payment, but buried feature, no rewards focus
  • Gap identified: No app designed specifically for credit card users with reward optimization

Decision: Clear opportunity in underserved, affluent segment willing to pay for better experience

TYPE 2: VALIDATION RESEARCH

"Will users actually use and pay for MY solution?"

Use this when:
  • You've defined your solution concept
  • Before building the full product (MVP validation)
  • Testing new features on existing product
  • Validating pricing strategy
What you're researching:
  • User behavior: Will they actually use this? How often?
  • Willingness to pay: At what price point? What's too expensive?
  • Feature prioritization: Which features matter most?
  • Usability: Can they actually use your solution?
  • Messaging: What positioning resonates with them?
üëì Example: Lenskart's Home Try-On Validation (2014)

The Hypothesis: "Users will buy eyewear online if they can try frames at home first"

Validation Test Design:
  • Test group: 500 users in Delhi and Mumbai
  • Process: Users select 3 frames, Lenskart ships them for free trial
  • Metrics: Conversion rate, return rate, customer satisfaction
  • Result: 35% conversion rate (vs 8% for online-only), 12% return rate (acceptable)
  • Decision: Scale home try-on to all cities, became core differentiator
