MODULE 3: DEFINING SOLUTIONS

From Problems to Possibilities

Week 3 Solution Definition Prioritization Roadmaps

Module Overview

What You'll Learn:

  • How to generate creative solutions without constraints
  • Methods to evaluate and prioritize solutions
  • Frameworks to validate and present solution ideas
  • How to build effective product roadmaps

What is a Solution?

The Simple Definition

A solution is a way to address a user's problem or need. It's the "how" that answers the "what" (the problem).

The Deeper Truth

A solution isn't just about fixing something broken. It's about:

  • Creating value for users
  • Meeting business objectives
  • Being feasible to build and deliver
  • Solving the right problem in the right way

Why This Matters

Many products fail not because they're poorly built, but because they solve the wrong problem or solve it in the wrong way. Great product managers master the art of solution definition.

Part 1: Being a Good Solutionist

Who is a Good Solutionist?

A good solutionist is someone who:

1. Thinks of Multiple Ways to Solve the Same Problem

  • Doesn't settle for the first idea
  • Explores diverse approaches
  • Considers solutions from different angles

2. Knows Applications of Technologies for Real World Problems

  • Understands what's technically possible
  • Connects tech capabilities with user needs
  • Stays updated on emerging solutions

3. Is Inclusive in Stakeholder Approach

  • Considers all affected parties
  • Designs for diverse user groups
  • Balances competing interests

4. Designs Solutions That Meet Team/Institution Objectives

  • Aligns with business goals
  • Considers resource constraints
  • Thinks about sustainability

ü§î Think About This:

Imagine you're solving "people get lost in large hospitals." A bad solutionist says "let's add signs." A good solutionist explores: digital wayfinding apps, companion volunteers, AR navigation, simplified layouts, better check-in processes, text message directions, voice guidance, AND signs. See the difference?

Part 2: Listing All Solutions

Purpose

Irrespective of constraints, think creatively at a high level about ALL possible solutions. This is your blue-sky thinking phase.

The "Let's" Framework Structure

Format:

Let's [build/do/offer] [key solution idea] so [problem it solves]

Why This Structure Works

  • "Let's" - Creates collaborative energy and action orientation
  • [build/do/offer] - Forces you to think about implementation type
  • [key solution idea] - Requires clarity and specificity
  • "so" - Demands you connect solution to problem
  • [problem it solves] - Keeps you focused on user value

Example: Food Delivery App Problem - "Users don't know when food will arrive"

Let's generate ALL possible solutions:

  1. Let's build a real-time GPS tracking feature so users can see exactly where their delivery person is
  2. Let's offer time-guarantee refunds so users feel compensated for uncertainty
  3. Let's do automated SMS updates every 5 minutes so users stay informed without checking the app
  4. Let's build an AI prediction model so users get accurate delivery estimates
  5. Let's offer a "delivery window" option so users can choose their preferred time slot
  6. Let's do driver check-ins at key milestones so users get status confirmations
  7. Let's build a chatbot so users can ask about delivery status anytime
  8. Let's offer live video streaming of food preparation so users see progress
  9. Let's do proactive delay notifications so users aren't surprised
  10. Let's build a rewards program for early deliveries so drivers are incentivized to be punctual

The Key Principle: No Idea is Too Wild

At this stage:

  • ‚ùå Don't judge feasibility
  • ‚ùå Don't worry about cost
  • ‚ùå Don't limit by technology
  • ‚úÖ Do think creatively
  • ‚úÖ Do consider diverse approaches
  • ‚úÖ Do quantity over quality

The "How Might We" Framework

What is HMW?

"How Might We" is a brainstorming technique that reframes problems into opportunities.

The Three Magic Words

  1. "How" - Suggests we don't have the answer yet (opens possibilities)
  2. "Might" - Suggests our ideas could work or might not (removes pressure)
  3. "We" - Suggests we'll solve it together (builds collaboration)

Converting Problems to HMW Questions

Problem Statement: "Users struggle with complex legal terminology"

Bad HMW: "How might we simplify legal language?"

  • Too obvious, doesn't inspire creativity

Good HMW: "How might we make legal concepts feel like everyday conversations?"

  • Opens creative possibilities

Great HMW: "How might we help users understand legal implications without reading legal jargon?"

  • Focuses on the outcome, not the method

HMW Practice Examples

Problem HMW Question
Elderly users find our app confusing How might we make our app feel familiar to people who didn't grow up with smartphones?
Users abandon checkout due to long forms How might we gather necessary information without it feeling like homework?
Customers don't trust our new brand How might we demonstrate our reliability before users make a purchase?

Part 3: MoSCoW Feature Categorization

What is MoSCoW?

MoSCoW is a prioritization method that categorizes features into four buckets.

The Four Categories

M - MUST Have

  • Critical features without which the product fails
  • Non-negotiable for launch
  • Legal requirements, core functionality
  • If we don't have this, users can't complete their primary goal

Example (Food Delivery App):

  • Menu browsing
  • Order placement
  • Payment processing
  • Delivery address entry

S - SHOULD Have

  • Important features that add significant value
  • Not critical for launch but needed soon after
  • Users will notice if missing but can work around it
  • Nice to have for launch, essential for success

Example:

  • Order history
  • Restaurant ratings
  • Estimated delivery time
  • Multiple payment options

C - COULD Have

  • Desirable features that improve experience
  • Can be postponed if resources are limited
  • Users won't complain if missing initially
  • Would be nice but not necessary

Example:

  • Loyalty rewards program
  • Social sharing of orders
  • Dietary filters
  • Restaurant recommendations

W - WON'T Have (this time)

  • Explicitly out of scope for current release
  • Might be considered for future versions
  • Helps manage stakeholder expectations
  • We've thought about it, but deliberately excluding it

Example:

  • Grocery delivery
  • Table reservations
  • Recipe suggestions
  • Meal planning tools

Why MoSCoW Matters

  1. Prevents Scope Creep - Clear boundaries on what's in/out
  2. Manages Expectations - Stakeholders know what to expect
  3. Enables Smart Tradeoffs - Clear on what can be cut if needed
  4. Speeds Decision Making - Framework for quick prioritization

üéØ The Real Skill: Knowing the Difference

Many PMs struggle because they categorize everything as "Must Have."

Ask These Questions:

  • Can users complete their core task without this?
  • Will the product be meaningless if we remove this?
  • Is this a regulatory/legal requirement?
  • Would users pay for the product without this feature?

If all answers are "yes" ‚Üí Must Have
If most are "yes" ‚Üí Should Have
If some are "yes" ‚Üí Could Have
If none are "yes" ‚Üí Won't Have

Part 4: RICE Framework

What is RICE?

RICE is a scoring method to prioritize solutions based on four factors:

  • Reach
  • Impact
  • Confidence
  • Effort

The Formula

RICE Score = (Reach √ó Impact √ó Confidence) / Effort

Higher score = Higher priority

Breaking Down Each Component

R - Reach

How many users will this impact in a given time period?

Measurement: Number of users/customers per time period (month/quarter)

Examples:

  • "This feature will reach 10,000 users per month"
  • "This will impact 50% of our user base in Q1"
  • "Every new user will encounter this (5,000/month)"

Common Mistake: Counting total users instead of who it reaches in a time period

I - Impact

How much will this impact each user when they encounter it?

Scale: Use a standardized scale

  • 3 = Massive impact
  • 2 = High impact
  • 1 = Medium impact
  • 0.5 = Low impact
  • 0.25 = Minimal impact

Examples:

  • Fixing a checkout bug (prevents purchase) = 3 (Massive)
  • Adding one-click reorder = 2 (High)
  • Improving search suggestions = 1 (Medium)
  • Changing button color = 0.5 (Low)
  • Adding animation to loading screen = 0.25 (Minimal)

Think About: Does this solve a major pain point? Does it enable a key outcome?

C - Confidence

How confident are you in your estimates?

Scale: Percentage

  • 100% = High confidence (have data/research)
  • 80% = Medium confidence (some data/research)
  • 50% = Low confidence (mostly gut feeling)

Examples:

  • "We've tested this with 50 users" = 100%
  • "We have competitor data and some user interviews" = 80%
  • "This is based on stakeholder opinion" = 50%

Pro Tip: Lower confidence = higher risk. Sometimes better to do research first.

E - Effort

How much work is required?

Measurement: Person-months (total team effort)

Examples:

  • Simple UI change = 0.5 person-months
  • New feature with backend + frontend = 3 person-months
  • Major platform overhaul = 12 person-months

Consider:

  • Design time
  • Development time
  • Testing time
  • Documentation time
  • Deployment complexity

üìà RICE in Action: Complete Example

Feature: Add real-time GPS tracking to food delivery app

Let's calculate:

  1. Reach: 50,000 orders per month will use this
    • R = 50,000
  2. Impact: Users constantly complain about not knowing delivery status; this solves major pain
    • I = 2 (High impact)
  3. Confidence: We've interviewed 30 users, all want this; competitors have it successfully
    • C = 80% (0.8)
  4. Effort: Need GPS integration, real-time updates, map display, backend infrastructure
    • E = 4 person-months

RICE Score = (50,000 √ó 2 √ó 0.8) / 4 = 20,000

Comparing Multiple Solutions

Solution Reach Impact Confidence Effort RICE Score Priority
GPS Tracking 50,000 2 0.8 4 20,000 ü•á 1st
Chatbot Support 10,000 1 0.5 3 1,667 3rd
Loyalty Program 30,000 1 0.8 6 4,000 ü•à 2nd
Video of Food Prep 5,000 0.5 0.5 8 156 4th

Insight: Even though video of food prep seems cool, its low reach and high effort make it a poor priority.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common RICE Mistakes

  1. Overestimating Impact - Being in love with your own idea
    • Fix: Ask "Would users pay extra for this?"
  2. Underestimating Effort - Forgetting testing, design, documentation
    • Fix: Include full development lifecycle
  3. Fake Confidence - Guessing but marking 100%
    • Fix: Be honest; low confidence is valuable information
  4. Wrong Time Period for Reach - Mixing daily and monthly numbers
    • Fix: Standardize to one period (usually monthly or quarterly)

Part 5: Lean Business Canvas

What is the Lean Business Canvas?

A one-page business plan that helps you present a solution comprehensively by breaking it down into 9 key building blocks.

Why Lean Canvas vs. Traditional Business Plan?

Traditional Business Plan Lean Canvas
50-100 pages 1 page
Takes weeks/months Takes hours
Based on assumptions Designed to test assumptions
Static document Living document
For investors For building products

The 9 Building Blocks (In Order of Completion)

Block 1: Problem (Complete This First)

What problem are you solving?

What to Include:

  • Top 3 problems your users face
  • Existing alternatives they currently use
  • Why existing alternatives fail

Example: Legal Tech App

Problems:

  1. High legal costs (‚Çπ5,000-50,000 per consultation)
  2. Complex legal language and terminology
  3. Reliance on internal references/connections

Existing Alternatives:

  • Hire expensive lawyers
  • Google legal information (unreliable)
  • Ask friends/family for recommendations
  • Legal aid clinics (long wait times)

Why Think About This First: Everything else on the canvas is meaningless if you're solving the wrong problem.

Block 2: Customer Segments (Complete This Second)

Who has the problem?

What to Include:

  • Who are your target customers?
  • Early adopters (first users)
  • Demographics and characteristics

Example: Legal Tech App

Target Customers:

  • Age: 16-50
  • Geography: Start Gujarat ‚Üí Western India ‚Üí Pan-India
  • People facing common legal issues (tenant disputes, consumer rights, traffic violations)
  • Tech-comfortable users who prefer digital solutions

Early Adopters:

  • Young professionals (25-35)
  • Small business owners
  • People with previous legal frustrations
  • Tech-savvy individuals

Why Identify Early Adopters: They're more forgiving of imperfect products and will give you feedback. Don't try to serve everyone at once.

Block 3: Solution (Complete This Third)

How do you solve the problem?

What to Include:

  • Top 3 features/solutions
  • How each addresses a specific problem

Example: Legal Tech App

Solutions:

  1. AI-Powered Chat Interface
    • Solves: Accessibility and cost (no expensive lawyer needed)
  2. Plain Language Translation
    • Solves: Complex legal jargon becomes understandable
  3. Mobile-First App
    • Solves: Access anytime, anywhere (no reliance on references)

Key Principle: Each solution should map to a specific problem from Block 1.

Block 4: Unique Value Proposition (UVP)

Why are you different and worth paying attention to?

What to Include:

  • Single, clear, compelling message
  • What makes you different from alternatives
  • Why customers should choose you

Formula:

[End benefit] for [target customer] who [problem/need]

Example: Legal Tech App

UVP:

"Legal clarity at your fingertips, in your language, at a fraction of the cost"

What Makes It Unique:

  • Unlike expensive lawyers ‚Üí Affordable
  • Unlike Google ‚Üí Reliable and personalized
  • Unlike competitors ‚Üí Multi-language support (key differentiator)

Bad UVP Examples:

  • "The best legal app" (not specific)
  • "AI-powered legal solutions" (technology, not benefit)
  • "Fast and easy" (too generic)

Good UVP Examples:

  • "Get personalized legal advice in Gujarati for ‚Çπ99"
  • "Legal solutions without the legal jargon"

Block 5: Unfair Advantage

What can't be easily copied or bought?

What to Include:

  • Real competitive advantages
  • Things that take time to build
  • Unique assets or capabilities

Example: Legal Tech App

Unfair Advantages:

  • Intellectual Property: Proprietary AI algorithm trained on regional laws
  • Design Excellence: User experience that makes legal solutions feel simple
  • Network Effects: As more users ask questions, AI gets smarter
  • Regional Expertise: Deep understanding of Gujarat legal system
  • Brand Reputation: (builds over time)

What is NOT an Unfair Advantage:

  • "We'll execute better" (everyone says this)
  • "First mover advantage" (rarely lasts)
  • "Better technology" (can be copied)
  • Money, team (can be hired/bought)

The Real Test: If a competitor with unlimited money tried to copy you tomorrow, what would they struggle with?

Block 6: Channels

How do you reach your customers?

What to Include:

  • How customers discover you
  • How you deliver your product
  • How you communicate with users
Types of Channels:

Owned Channels:

  • Your website
  • Email list
  • Mobile app
  • Blog/content

Earned Channels:

  • Social media (organic)
  • Word of mouth
  • PR/Media coverage
  • User referrals

Paid Channels:

  • Social media ads
  • Search engine marketing (SEM)
  • Display advertising
  • Influencer partnerships

Block 7: Key Metrics

How do you measure success?

The Pirate Metrics Framework (AARRR):

  1. Acquisition: How many users discover you?
  2. Activation: Do they have a good first experience?
  3. Retention: Do they come back?
  4. Revenue: Are they paying?
  5. Referral: Do they tell others?

Block 8: Cost Structure

What will it cost to create and deliver your solution?

Types of Costs:

Fixed Costs (Same regardless of users):

  • Team salaries
  • Office rent
  • Software licenses (fixed fee)
  • Legal/compliance
  • Marketing (brand building)

Variable Costs (Increase with more users):

  • Server/hosting costs
  • Customer support
  • Transaction fees
  • AI API calls
  • Marketing (per acquisition)

Block 9: Revenue Streams

How do you make money?

Common Revenue Models:

  1. Subscription (Recurring) - Monthly/annual fees
  2. Transaction Fee - Charge per use
  3. Freemium - Basic features free, premium features paid
  4. Advertising - Display ads, sponsored content
  5. Marketplace - Commission on transactions

üéØ How to Use the Lean Canvas

  1. Fill it out in order: Problem ‚Üí Customer Segments ‚Üí Solution ‚Üí UVP ‚Üí Unfair Advantage ‚Üí Channels ‚Üí Key Metrics ‚Üí Costs ‚Üí Revenue
  2. Treat it as hypotheses, not facts: Everything is an assumption until validated
  3. Update regularly: Review monthly, update as you learn
  4. Test risky assumptions first: Identify biggest uncertainties, run experiments to validate

Part 6: Solution Hypothesis Statement

What is a Solution Hypothesis?

A testable statement that clearly defines what you're building, who it's for, and why you believe it will work.

Purpose

  1. Forces Clarity - Makes you articulate your assumptions
  2. Enables Testing - Turns beliefs into testable hypotheses
  3. Creates Alignment - Gets team on same page
  4. Guides Development - Provides clear direction

The Structure

We believe that by [offering/building/doing solution idea],
[user group] will [experience change or benefit],
because [reason/insight].

üåü Examples: From Bad to Great

Example 1: Food Delivery App

‚ùå Bad:

"We believe that by building a food delivery app, users will order more food, because everyone likes convenience."

  • Too generic
  • No specific user group
  • Weak reasoning

‚úÖ Good:

"We believe that by offering real-time GPS tracking of delivery drivers, busy professionals will feel more in control of their time, because our research shows uncertainty about delivery timing is the #1 source of frustration."

  • Specific solution
  • Defined user group
  • Clear benefit
  • Research-based reasoning

‚≠ê Great:

"We believe that by building a GPS tracking feature with 'ready in 5 minutes' notifications, busy professionals ordering during work breaks will order 30% more frequently, because our interviews revealed that fear of food arriving during meetings is the primary reason users don't order more often."

  • Very specific solution
  • Precise user context
  • Measurable outcome
  • Validated insight

üî¨ How to Test Your Hypothesis

  1. Identify the Riskiest Assumption - What if this isn't true?
  2. Design a Test - User interviews, MVP, surveys
  3. Learn and Iterate - Validate, partially validate, or invalidate

üö® Common Mistakes in Hypothesis Writing

Mistake 1: Solution Looking for a Problem

‚ùå: "We believe that by using blockchain technology, millennials will trust our platform more, because blockchain is the future."

‚úÖ: "We believe that by showing transparent transaction history with tamper-proof records, gig economy workers will choose our payment platform, because 35% report disputes over payment amounts with no proof."

Mistake 2: Too Vague to Test

‚ùå: "We believe that by making our app better, users will be happier, because good UX matters."

‚úÖ: "We believe that by reducing checkout steps from 5 to 2, mobile users will increase purchase completion by 25%, because our analytics show 60% abandon cart at step 3."

Part 7: Product Roadmap

What is a Product Roadmap?

A strategic document that shows:

  • What you're building
  • Why you're building it
  • When you're building it (roughly)
  • How it aligns with company goals

What a Roadmap is NOT

  • ‚ùå A list of features
  • ‚ùå A detailed project plan
  • ‚ùå A commitment of exact dates
  • ‚ùå A wish list from stakeholders

Why Roadmaps Matter

  1. Alignment - Gets everyone moving in the same direction
  2. Communication - Clear vision of the future
  3. Prioritization - Shows what matters most
  4. Expectation Management - Shows what's NOT being built
  5. Strategic Thinking - Forces long-term planning

üèóÔ∏è How to Build a Product Roadmap

Step 1: Define Vision & Goals

Vision: The big picture - where are you going?

Goals: Specific objectives that support the vision

Step 2: Prioritize Based on Impact & Effort

Use the frameworks you've learned: RICE scoring, MoSCoW, Value vs. Effort Matrix

Step 3: Organize into Phases/Milestones

Common Roadmap Structures:

  • Time-Based: Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4
  • Now/Next/Later: Currently building, up next, future consideration
  • Theme-Based: Organized by strategic themes

üìÖ Example Roadmap: Food Delivery App

Vision: Most reliable food delivery platform in India

Now (Next 2 Months) - Theme: Trust & Reliability

Goal: Increase delivery accuracy to 90%

Initiative Why Expected Outcome
GPS Tracking Users constantly ask "where's my food?" 40% reduction in "where is my order" support tickets
Smart ETA Algorithm Current estimates are off by 15-20 mins Improve accuracy from 75% to 90%
Driver Check-Ins Validate delivery milestones Real-time status updates, fewer customer calls

üéØ PM Pro Tips for Roadmaps

Tip 1: Keep Roadmaps Outcome-Focused, Not Feature-Focused

‚ùå Feature-Focused (Bad):

Q1: Build GPS tracking, notifications, dark mode

‚úÖ Outcome-Focused (Good):

Q1: Improve Delivery Reliability

  • Users will know exactly when food arrives (GPS tracking)
  • Users stay informed automatically (smart notifications)

Target: 90% delivery accuracy, -40% support tickets

Tip 2: Use Time Horizons (Now/Next/Later) Instead of Specific Dates

The 70-20-10 Rule:

  • 70% - Core product improvements
  • 20% - Adjacent opportunities
  • 10% - Transformational bets

Part 8: Real-World Solution Selection

In Real World, A Solution Must Pass These Tests:

Test 1: Primary Stakeholder Buy-In

The main person/group affected by the solution must want it.

Example: B2B School Management Software

Primary Stakeholder: Teachers (main users)

‚ùå Fails Test: "Let's add automated grading that teachers can't override"

‚úÖ Passes Test: "Let's add AI-assisted grading that teachers can review and adjust"

Test 2: Institutional Capability

Can your organization actually build and maintain this?

Consider:

  • Technical capability - Do we have the skills?
  • Resource availability - Do we have time/budget?
  • Operational capacity - Can we support it long-term?
  • Build vs. Buy vs. Partner - What's most realistic?

Test 3: Core Institution Objectives

Does this solution help achieve business goals (usually profitability, growth, mission)?

Test 4: Product-Market Fit

Do customers actually want this badly enough to use/pay for it?

‚úÖ Strong PMF Signals:

  • Users actively seek you out
  • High organic growth (word of mouth)
  • Users disappointed if product disappeared
  • High engagement and retention

The Sean Ellis Test:

Ask users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"

  • >40% say "Very disappointed" ‚Üí Strong PMF ‚úÖ
  • <40% say "Very disappointed" ‚Üí Weak PMF ‚ùå

Test 5: SMART Goals

Solutions must be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound

‚ùå Not SMART: "Make the app faster"

‚úÖ SMART: "Reduce checkout page load time from 3.2 seconds to under 2 seconds for 95% of users by end of Q2, by implementing image compression and code splitting"

üéØ Putting It All Together: The 5-Test Filter

Use this as a checklist for EVERY solution you consider:

  • ‚ñ° Test 1: Do primary stakeholders want this?
  • ‚ñ° Test 2: Can we actually build/maintain this?
  • ‚ñ° Test 3: Does this serve our core objectives?
  • ‚ñ° Test 4: Is there product-market fit?
  • ‚ñ° Test 5: Is this SMART?

Passes all 5 tests? ‚Üí PROCEED ‚úÖ
Fails any test? ‚Üí REVISE or REJECT ‚ùå

What Makes a Good Solution?

  1. Creative & Comprehensive - Consider all possibilities first
  2. Evidence-Based - Validated through research and testing
  3. User-Centered - Solves real problems for real people
  4. Strategically Aligned - Serves business objectives
  5. Realistically Feasible - Within capability to deliver
  6. Measurably Valuable - Has clear success metrics

The Solution Definition Process

  1. Generate Many Solutions (HMW, "Let's" Framework)
  2. Prioritize (RICE, MoSCoW)
  3. Validate (Lean Canvas, Hypothesis Testing)
  4. Plan (Roadmap, SMART Goals)
  5. Test Reality (5-Test Filter)
  6. Build & Learn

Golden Rules for Solutions

  1. Fall in love with the problem, not your solution
  2. Solutions are hypotheses until validated
  3. Perfect is the enemy of good
  4. Think big, start small
  5. Users don't want features, they want outcomes
